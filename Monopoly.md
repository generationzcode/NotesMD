- When there's just one firm in a market and it sets the price for the quantity demanded. 
- Now with competitive markets, the price is set for a company by the economy and the quantity it produces is a maximization of the company's profits to that price. Since $Revenue=QP$ and cost is whatever the cost function is to produce that good, we get the maximization as $MR=MC$, which is $P=MC$. 
- Why does this price settle to the point where the demand curve meets MR? If the price we take and get the quantity supplied with the marginal cost curve, then cross check with the demand curve, we'll find 3 situations:
	- The price shown in the demand curve for the same quantity would be higher. This means the firm can make more money by producing more.
	- The price shown in the demand curve for the same quantity is lower.  This means the final price the firm must "take" is lower than what we initially took and profit maximized for. They'll be earning subpar profits.
	- The price is the same as the demand curve for the profit maximization. This means we were spot on.

- For monopolies, they don't need to be pushed around by the larger industry, so their profit maximization looks different to price takers.
- As we can see with price takers, they are really just given a price that the market is accepting for their product and they must make the most money given that constraint. A monopoly is given a demand curve and given a marginal cost curve.
- When a monopoly considers production, they want to sell their stuff to get the most profit. If they sell at too high a price, few people will buy, so the total profit wont be too much. At the other end, if they sell at the point at which average price equals marginal cost, they'll be making a lot of revenue, but since they need to sell their entire quantity at the same price, they'll be making less money. The perfect scale of operation is decided by the $MC=MR$ point.
