> An analysis of the science and math behind drying
## Temperatures Inside
- The temperature of the solids in dryers quickly goes up from $T_o$ to $T_v$, vaporization temperature
- The vaporization temperature differs from dryer to dryer. In direct heating dryers, it's at the boiling point of the liquid since the vapour above it is saturated
- In indirect dryers, it's at the wet bulb temperature of the gas being used.
>[!FAQ] What is Wet Bulb????
>The wet bulb temperature is the temperature where the liquid in an object is being evaporated and the latent heat is being supplied by the temperature difference of the liquid to the gas surrounding it. 
- Drying occurs at T_v for almost all of the drying period. Towards the end, it goes higher

## Mass transfer
- Most dryers are controlled by resistance to mass transfer
- ![[Pasted image 20250326143123.png]]
- the above is the rate of mass transfer
- The humidity of air affects the mass transfer of the liquid
- This can be seen from the moisture humidity equilibrium data of the material. Some materials hold the liquid better than others, so for a certain moisture content, they have a lower humidity
- If the humidity of the gas is below the humidity of the moisture humidity curve, the gas removes moisture from the solid, if not then it's the other way around
![[Pasted image 20250326143536.png]]
- Bound and unbound water - when the material contains more liquid than the amount shown by the graph at 100% RH, the amount of water as shown in the graph that exists in the material exerts a smaller vapour pressure than what is expected - it is thus called bound water (in capillaries and such). The rest is unbound. Eg Soap at 100% RH having 30 pounds per 100 pounds water content. The bound water is 26% and unbound 4%
