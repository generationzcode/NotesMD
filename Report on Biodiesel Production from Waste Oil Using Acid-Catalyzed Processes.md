

**Introduction**  
Biodiesel production from waste oil aligns with the "Repurpose Used Cooking Oil" (RUCO) initiative, addressing both waste management and renewable energy needs. Biodiesel, comprised of fatty acid alkyl esters, is commonly produced via transesterification, a reaction of oils with alcohols (e.g., methanol). Acid-catalyzed processes are particularly suitable for waste oils with high free fatty acid (FFA) content, avoiding the need for extensive pretreatment.

The proposed project focuses on designing and implementing a biodiesel production plant utilizing transesterification of waste cooking oils (WCO) and other diverse feedstocks such as inedible oils, vegetable oils, and oils sourced from the food industry, restaurants, and households. With a production capacity of 50,000 liters of biodiesel per day, the plant aims to process 100 tons of feedstock daily, addressing the challenges posed by varying feed compositions, impurities, and contaminants through innovative preprocessing techniques and robust design.


___


**Process Description**: 

The preprocessing phase ensures feedstock consistency by employing filtration to remove solids, acid esterification to reduce free fatty acid (FFA) content in high-FFA oils, and vacuum drying to eliminate moisture, thereby mitigating soap formation during transesterification. The heart of the process, the transesterification reaction, is conducted in a continuous stirred-tank reactor (CSTR) at optimized conditions (60–65°C, 1–3 bar), using a stoichiometric excess of methanol (6:1 molar ratio to oil) and tailored catalysts to maximize conversion rates.
Catalyst selection is tailored to feedstock characteristics. Homogeneous catalysts such as sodium hydroxide (NaOH) are utilized for low-FFA feedstocks, while heterogeneous catalysts like calcium oxide (CaO) are employed for high-FFA oils to enhance separation efficiency and reusability. Enzymatic catalysts (lipase) are considered for specific cases where low temperatures and environmentally friendly processes are prioritized. The process is supported by real-time feedstock analysis using advanced sensors to adjust methanol and catalyst dosing dynamically, ensuring optimal reaction conditions for varied feed compositions.
From an environmental perspective, the project targets significant GHG emission reductions, with a detailed assessment of CO2 emissions per liter or megajoule (MJ) of biodiesel. Biodiesel's energy return on investment (EROI) will be analyzed by comparing the energy required for production with the energy output of the fuel, emphasizing its viability as a renewable alternative.
Post-reaction, glycerol is separated via centrifugation, and biodiesel undergoes a multi-stage purification process, including water washing and vacuum drying, to meet ASTM D6751 and EN 14214 standards. The plant design integrates energy recovery systems, utilizing waste heat from separation units to preheat incoming feedstock and methanol, significantly reducing overall energy consumption. The energy required per liter of biodiesel (1–2 MJ) is substantially lower than the energy content of biodiesel (37 MJ/L), highlighting its efficiency.
This plant is designed to adapt dynamically to feedstock variability, supported by a robust process control system. Through innovative engineering, rigorous optimization, and sustainable practices, the project not only transforms waste into valuable biofuels but also establishes a scalable model for renewable energy production.

Acid-catalyzed biodiesel production from waste oil is technologically viable and complements RUCO's objectives.
