## When Wine Spoils - Vinegar
- Conversion of alcohol in wine to acetic acid by acetobacter spp
- It contains other organic acids as well: malic, succinic, and lactic acids
### How it's made
- produced by 2 stage fermentation of raw materials containing starch or sugar
- distilled vinegar. This usually uses corn or barley in fermentation
- balsamic vinegar - starts from cooked grapes that are fermented
- wine vinegar - uses wine in the second stage fermentation
- apple cider - no clue
### Bacteria Used
- acetobacter xylinum, europaeus, vinellandii
- glucobacter oxydans
- These should be able to survive at low pH and high concentrations of acetic acid
- They should not oxidise acetic acid further and form $CO_2$
### Material Balance
- a material balance of what happens:
- 1 mole of glucose -> 2 moles of alcohol
- 1 mole of alcohol -> 1 mole of acetic acid
- so 1 gram of alcohol gives 1.3 grams of acetic acid
- The above is the max yield. So there's a yield factor which is always less than 1, since all the carbon doesn't go to the desired reactions
### Methods to prepare vinegar
- Slow vinegar method (orleans method): put wine in an open vat. Air and bacteria convert wine to vinegar. Then it's put in wooden casks, left in the field for 4-5 weeks. Casks filled to 2/3 capacity. Small amount of vinegar is added to cask to lower pH and inhibit bacteria growth
#### Trickling Generator Method
- alcoholic liquid sprinkled at the top
- Air enters through the middle section
- Beech woord shavings or other packings contain acetic acid bacteria
- as the alcohol flows down, the bacteria oxidises it to acetic acid

#### Submerged Fermentation
- a stirred medium containing alcohol has some bacteria added to it and temperature is controlled inside it
- Bacteria grow in a suspension of fine air bubbles and fermenting alcohol. The suspension is produced by an aerator
- This is a 3 phase reactor

- most common method for fermentation
- Aeration must be vigorous as shortage of oxygen because of the highly acidic conditions
