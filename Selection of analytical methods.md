* analyte is the thing you're looking for, sample is the thing from which youre finding the analyte
* 1$\mu$gram/L is ppb, 1mg/L is ppm concentration

> Accuracy is how closely the result agrees with the "true" or expected result. We can express accuracy as absolute error
> $$S=KC$$ where S is the signal, K is the constant and C is the concentration
 K is the sensitivity constant

Methods involving total analysis like gravimetry and titration have high accuracy

> Precision of analytical data is the degree of mutual agreement among data that have been obtained the same way
> Precision is measured by the random,indeterminate error in the analysis

> Sensitivity of an instrument is a measure of it's ability to distinguish small changes in signal or analyte concentration

> Specificity of an instrument is when you get signal only from the analyte no other source
> Selectivity of a method is $K = \frac{k_1}{k_2}$, where k1 and k2 are the sensitivity constants

>A researcher describes fluorosence method for the analysis of ${Ag}^+$ in water. When analysing a solution that contains $10^{-9}M$ of ${Ag}^+$ and $1.1*10^{-7}M$ of ${Ni}^{2+}$. The fluorosence intensity was 4.9% greater than that obtained for a solution of ${Ag}^+$. What is the selectivity coefficient of given analytical method?

### Solution
$$S/C = k$$
$$1/\frac{S*1.1*10^{-7}}{0.049S*10^{-9}}$$
$$K = 4.5*10^{-7}$$

## Equipment time and cost
* Methods relying on instrumentation are equipment intensive and require training. They are also pretty expensive. 
* cost of an analysis ends up depending on cost of equipment, cost of hiring analysis and number of samples that can be processed per hour
## Robust 
* If the method is free from any kind of chemical interferences
> [! Example]

* Sensitivity is for the instrument and robustness can be for an instrument
