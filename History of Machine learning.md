- It all started at Dartmouth college, summer of 1956. This was done after computer scientists had a good grip on the low level tasks achievable by computers
- They defined their purpose:
>[!Quote]
>The study is to proceed on the basis of the conjecture that every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can in principle be described to precisely that a machine can simulate it. An attempt will be made to find how to make machines:
>- use language
>- form abstractions and concepts
>- solve all kinds of problems now reserved for humans
>- improve themselves

- People in this workshop: invented genetic algorithms, information and communication theory, invented LISP, algorithmic probability, etc.
- Turing wrote a nice paper on the idea of whether machines can think or not, [Machine Learning Turing](https://courses.cs.umbc.edu/471/papers/turing.pdf)
- John McCarthy created LISP, a language based on lambda calculus. It's the second oldest high level language, a year younger than Fortran
- In 1959, Simon, Newell and Shaw created the general problem solver, which in theory can solve any well formed equation
- Arthur Samuel made a program that played checkers, he coined the term, "Machine Learning"
- Marvin Minsky and Dean made SNARC, the first neural net (Wasn't Minsky against neural nets???)
- John Holland made genetic algorithms 1960 (I thought it was 1975 but nevermind)
