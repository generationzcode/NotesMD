## Dispersion Stabilizers
Anionic surfactants (?) are used at 1-5 wt%  of the monomer. they have hydrophobic hydrocarbon molecules on one end and a hydrophillic anionic head group. These surfactant molecules, when dissolved in water, above their "critical micelle concentration", begin associating into groups of spherical aggregates called micelles, which contain about 100 molecules. 
These micelles contain a hydrophobic core, which can absorb a good amount of water insoluble substances

When monomer is added, 3 phases develop - aqueous phase with small amounts of monomer and surfactant, large droplets containing lots of monomer and surfactant, small droplets (very very common) containing some monomer and surfactant around 

## Steps of emulsion polymerization
### Particle nucleation
This is the stage where particles are formed. What are particles? The book doesn't say - I guess they are drops but polymer. Particle nucleation occurs through three methods:
* Micellular Nucleation - Micelle receives oligomeric radical formed from initiator and monomer in aqueous phase. Micelle keeps growing and attracting more monomer until no more micelles left without radical.
* Homogeneous nucleation - chain radical formed, attracted surfactant for colloidal stability and monomers for propagation. Ends when these particles get big enough to attract even oligomeric radicals
* Coagulation nucleation - A bunch of oligomeric radicals clump up together until the molecule is big enough to achieve colloidal stability itself and then starts absorbing monomer and propagates
Micellular nucleation happens when the solution is well above CMC, homogeneous is when surfactant is little and coagulation is when surfactant is even less
### Particle Growth
in the first part of particle growth (AKA interval 2), monomers are provided by monomer droplets to the particles, to keep them propagating. When all the monomer droplets are used up, we enter interval 3, when rate of polymerization continuously decreases

## Miniemulsion Polymerization
Developed with the objective of controlling number of particles. A miniemulsion is formed with droplets large in number and size to capture all radicals in the solution. The number of droplets are the number of particles
