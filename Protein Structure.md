- Confirmation of a protein is the spatial arrangement of atoms that depends on bonds and bond rotations
- Proteins have a native conformation, which is changes on denaturing
- native protein is folded through weak interactions: hydrophobic effect, hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds and van der waals forces
- The amide bond has a 40% double bond character
- Normally in a polypeptide chain, when you add up amino acids, you get a trans form. Only two amino acids allow for cis form - proline and glycine
- Torsion angles in chain:
- The alpha carbon and N $C_{\alpha-N}$  is called $\phi$
- The alpha carbon and the C $C_{\alpha-C}$ is called $\psi$
- The values of phi and psi are fixed numbers for different types of structures are fixed values

## Alpha helix
- it's a spiral, right handed coil. IT has the most favorable phi and psi angles with little steric hindrance
- repeated hydrogen bonds stabilise the structure
- number of repeating units per turn is 3.6 residues with a pitch of 5.4 angstrom
- The C=0 of the $n^{th}$ residue points towards the N-H of the $(n+4)^{th}$ residue
- ![[Pasted image 20250814110521.png]]
- These are arranged in bundles called alpha helical bundles. This is the tertiary structure
## Beta sheets
- There a number of polypeptide chains arranged in parallel kept together using hydrogen bonding
- ![[Pasted image 20250814110919.png]]
- They can also be arranged in antiparallel arrangement
- The parallel arrangement has both strands heading from C to N from left to right or right to left. Antiparallel is when one does not follow
- There can be any number of strands
- the difference between two pleats is about 7 angstrom![[Pasted image 20250814111135.png]]
- the pleats are like planes
- connection between adjacent sheets to form a tertiary structure requires a hairpin turn for antiparallel
## Motifs
- These are very commonly found ways that secondary structures are connected to each other
- examples of motifs are the 4 helix bundle, greek key and rossman fold
